Literature Survey

Sign languages, integral to the deaf community, vary globally, yet play a vital role in bridging communication gaps. American Sign Language (ASL), being the third most prevalent language in the U.S., boasts over 4,400 distinct signs. Meanwhile, Slovenian sign language comprises roughly 4,000 common signs. The nuances in sign languages extend beyond hand gestures, with facial expressions holding significant semantic value. Though fingerspelling is used for specific terms, most communication utilizes full words, paralleling the pace of spoken language. For the deaf community, sign language is paramount, hence the growing interest in sign language generation systems. An academic review spanning 1998-2020 was conducted on this topic, yielding 162 relevant studies out of 414, all highlighting the advancement and potential of these systems.
A focus on Arabic-speaking nations revealed a unique scenario. Despite varying spoken dialects across the 25 Arabic-speaking countries, a consistent Arabic alphabet is maintained. This implies the use of a unified Arabic Sign Language (ArSL). In these nations, the deaf community is notably isolated. ArSL, similar to other sign languages, uses hand shapes, movements, and facial expressions for communication. Addressing this, a proposed system aims to virtualize the translation of ArSL. It plans to harness 3D gestures recorded using data gloves and use graphical techniques to represent these gestures digitally. Sphinx-4, a speech recognition engine, will aid in translating common words directly, bypassing text-based conversion. This endeavor underscores the significance and potential of advanced sign language generation systems in enhancing communication for the deaf community across varied linguistic landscapes.

Research Problem & Solution

Do all the hearing impaired and related people in Sri Lanka, aware of these communication applications?

Language and communication form the bedrock of human interaction, yet for Sinhala sign language users, challenges abound. A prominent barrier arises when they attempt to communicate with individuals who do not speak Sinhala, underscoring the broader difficulties posed by linguistic differences. Furthermore, deaf individuals often grapple with accessing information that's predominantly catered to the hearing population, presented in written or spoken formats. This access barrier is compounded by the inherent communication challenges they face. Being heavily reliant on sign language due to their hearing and speech limitations, they find it difficult to seamlessly interact with the wider hearing community. This underscores the pressing need for more inclusive communication platforms and strategies to bridge these gaps.

Solution

To bridge the communication gap between deaf individuals and those unfamiliar with Sri Lankan sign language, a comprehensive digital tool is proposed. This innovative solution will convert English voice and text inputs into a 3D avatar that performs the corresponding Sri Lankan sign language, providing an interactive and visually engaging translation for users. Additionally, for native Sinhala speakers, the tool will transform Sinhala voice and text inputs into clear and illustrative images that depict the relevant Sri Lankan sign language gestures, facilitating a more intuitive understanding for quick interactions. Furthermore, enhancing two-way communication, the system will employ video recognition technology to interpret Sri Lankan sign language from video inputs, translating these gestures into coherent written text. Through this multifaceted approach, the tool promises to foster inclusivity, ensuring that every individual, irrespective of their hearing abilities or language proficiency, can communicate effectively and effortlessly.

Methodology

The mobile application is tailored for both hearing and non-hearing users to enhance communication via three pivotal features, all underpinned by Python-based machine learning on Azure Web Services and FLASK API. Firstly, the app transforms English voice or text inputs into a 3D avatar showcasing Sri Lankan sign language, utilizing manually curated datasets and an Artificial Neural Network (ANN). This involves processing 200 sign movements, refining English audio inputs, and leveraging the Google Speech-to-Text API for real-time translation. Secondly, Sinhala voice or text is translated into Sri Lankan sign language visuals. A collection of 500 images serves as the basis, which, after normalization and text preprocessing, feeds into another ANN model that synchronizes with the Google Speech-to-Text API for on-the-spot Sinhala to text translation. Lastly, the system provides a real-time conversion of Sri Lankan sign language to text. This is achieved through recording Sri Lankan sign gestures, using videos as training data, which are then processed and analyzed with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model, VGG19. Transfer learning bolsters the image categorization process, ensuring the most accurate sign-to-text translations. This amalgamation of technologies promises seamless integration between varied forms of communication, bridging the gap between different user types.
